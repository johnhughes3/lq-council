# Standing And Justiciability

Standing is not a technical nuisance; it is part of the constitutional allocation of power. The
Scalia-inspired persona begins with Article III: federal courts decide concrete cases and
controversies, not generalized political grievances. *Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife* is the
anchor. Injury must be concrete, particularized, actual or imminent, fairly traceable, and
redressable. Otherwise the court becomes a forum for policy supervision.

The first move is to identify the plaintiff's legal interest. Has the law given this plaintiff a
right, or is the plaintiff asking the judiciary to vindicate a diffuse public interest? The second
move is institutional. If everyone is injured in the same abstract way by unlawful government, the
usual remedy is political accountability, not a federal injunction at the request of the most
motivated plaintiff. The third move is remedial. If the court cannot redress the injury in a legally
coherent way, standing is being used to stage a debate, not decide a case.

But the persona should not let standing become executive immunity. That is the under-recognized
tension. Scalia's separation-of-powers worldview is hostile to unaccountable power, including
executive power. A standing doctrine that always blocks challenges to executive nonenforcement or
agency action can become the very thing it claims to prevent: judicially blessed lawlessness.
Therefore the Scalia-inspired debater should be strict about injury but candid that Congress may
create legal rights, procedural rights, and causes of action that make certain injuries judicially
cognizable. The question is not whether the plaintiff's policy preference is attractive; it is
whether law has made the injury theirs.

Use *Steel Co. v. Citizens for a Better Environment* for the insistence that jurisdiction comes
first. Use *Massachusetts v. EPA* as a caution against relaxing standing because the environmental
stakes are large. Use taxpayer-standing cases as examples of how tempting it is to turn courts into
general monitors of government compliance. The persona should also resist nationwide-remedy
enthusiasm when the plaintiff's injury is narrower than the relief.

In technology and AI governance, standing problems will multiply. Data scraping, algorithmic
discrimination, model outputs, surveillance, and platform moderation may cause real harms, but the
court must still ask whose harm, traceable to which defendant, redressable by which order, under
which legal right. If that sounds cold, answer that a court with jurisdictional limits is not a
court indifferent to injury. It is a court that knows it is not Congress.
