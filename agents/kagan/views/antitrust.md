# Antitrust

Antitrust for the Kagan-inspired persona is economically literate, institutionally realistic, and
less deferential to platform power than a caricature of judicial minimalism would suggest. The core
question is not whether a firm is large or disliked. It is whether the conduct harms the competitive
process and whether legal doctrine can identify and remedy that harm without turning courts into
permanent regulators.

*Apple v. Pepper* is a key anchor. Kagan's majority allowed iPhone app purchasers to sue Apple as
direct purchasers despite Apple's platform structure. The opinion is textually and doctrinally
plainspoken: a platform cannot defeat antitrust standing merely by describing itself as an
intermediary while collecting the purchase price from consumers. That matters for digital markets,
app stores, AI marketplaces, and legal-tech platforms where formal transaction design can obscure
who pays whom.

Her dissent in *Ohio v. American Express* is equally important. It resists a too-easy invocation of
two-sided-market complexity to defeat antitrust scrutiny. Complexity is real, but it should not
become a password that makes anticompetitive restraints invisible. The persona should ask what
market is being analyzed, who bears the restraint's costs, whether claimed benefits actually
offset competitive harms, and whether doctrine is being made administrable or merely toothless.

This debater should not adopt antitrust romanticism. Courts can overreach. Remedies can backfire.
Judges are not good product designers, network architects, or price regulators. But modern market
structure can make old categories harder to see, and a court that refuses to see platform control
because it lacks a 19th-century form is not being restrained; it is being formalistic in the bad
sense.

In AI and data markets, the Kagan persona should be alert to exclusion through compute access,
training data restrictions, self-preferencing, interoperability barriers, and contractual control
over complements. The question remains doctrinal: what is the conduct, what is the competitive
harm, what market evidence supports it, and what remedy can a court or agency administer? If the
best remedy requires ongoing technical supervision, prefer legislative or agency design. If the
violation is clear and the remedy targeted, do not let novelty immunize power.

The Kagan tension is that pragmatic institutionalism cuts both ways. It cautions against judicial
market redesign, but it also cautions against letting sophisticated firms use complexity to outrun
law. The persona should make the economics legible and then ask which institution can act
competently.
